Introduction & Theory

The Pythagorean Theorem (or Pythagoras' Theorem) is a fundamental principle in geometry relating the three sides of a right-angled triangle.

A right-angled triangle is any triangle that has one angle equal to exactly 90 degrees. The side opposite the right angle is called the hypotenuse, and it's always the longest side. The other two sides are called the legs (or sometimes 'cathetus').

The theorem states that the square of the length of the hypotenuse (c) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the two legs (a and b).

a² + b² = c²

Where:

  • a and b are the lengths of the legs of the right-angled triangle.
  • c is the length of the hypotenuse.

Important: This theorem *only* works for right-angled triangles!

Examples

Example 1: Finding the Hypotenuse

Imagine a right-angled triangle where leg a = 3 units and leg b = 4 units. We want to find the length of the hypotenuse c.

Using the formula: a² + b² = c²

Substitute the known values:

3² + 4² = c²

9 + 16 = c²

25 = c²

To find c, we take the square root of both sides:

c = √25

c = 5

So, the hypotenuse is 5 units long.

Example 2: Finding a Leg

Suppose we have a right-angled triangle with a hypotenuse c = 10 units and one leg a = 6 units. We need to find the length of the other leg b.

Start with the formula: a² + b² = c²

Substitute the known values:

6² + b² = 10²

36 + b² = 100

To find b², subtract 36 from both sides:

b² = 100 - 36

b² = 64

Now, take the square root of both sides:

b = √64

b = 8

So, the missing leg is 8 units long.

A Brief History

While named after the ancient Greek mathematician Pythagoras (around 570 – 495 BC), the relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle was known long before him.

  • Babylonians: Clay tablets, like Plimpton 322 (dating back to around 1800 BC), show lists of Pythagorean triples, indicating they understood the concept.
  • Egyptians: Evidence suggests they used ropes with knots (e.g., 3, 4, and 5 units apart) to create accurate right angles for construction, implying practical knowledge of the 3-4-5 triangle.
  • Pythagoras: Pythagoras or his school are often credited with the first formal proof of the theorem, establishing it as a fundamental principle of mathematics.

Proof of the Theorem (Proof by Rearrangement)

There are many ways to prove the Pythagorean theorem. One common visual proof involves rearranging four identical right-angled triangles.

Imagine a large square with side length (a + b). Inside this square, arrange four copies of a right-angled triangle with legs a and b and hypotenuse c such that their corners touch the corners of the large square. This leaves a smaller, tilted square in the center with side length c.

The area of the large square is (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b².

The area of the large square can also be calculated as the sum of the areas of the four triangles and the central square: Area = 4 * (½ * a * b) + c² = 2ab + c²

Since both expressions represent the area of the same large square, we can set them equal: a² + 2ab + b² = 2ab + c²

Subtracting 2ab from both sides leaves us with the theorem:

a² + b² = c²

(Visual diagrams often help understand this proof - consider searching for "Pythagorean theorem proof rearrangement" online.)

The Converse of the Theorem

The converse of the Pythagorean theorem is also true and very useful. It states:

If the square of the length of the longest side of a triangle is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides, then the triangle is a right-angled triangle.

In other words, if you have a triangle with sides a, b, and c (where c is the longest side) and you find that a² + b² = c², then you know for sure that the angle opposite side c is a right angle (90°).

Example: Checking for a Right Triangle

Is a triangle with sides 8, 15, and 17 a right-angled triangle?

The longest side is 17, so let c = 17. Let a = 8 and b = 15.

Check if a² + b² = c²:

8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289

17² = 289

Since 289 = 289, the condition is met. Therefore, the triangle is a right-angled triangle.

Pythagorean Triples

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three positive integers a, b, and c, such that a² + b² = c². These represent the side lengths of a right-angled triangle where all sides are whole numbers.

Common examples include:

  • (3, 4, 5) → 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5²
  • (5, 12, 13) → 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13²
  • (8, 15, 17) → 8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17²
  • (7, 24, 25) → 7² + 24² = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25²

Any multiple of a Pythagorean triple is also a Pythagorean triple. For example, since (3, 4, 5) is a triple, so are (6, 8, 10), (9, 12, 15), etc.

Triples where a, b, and c have no common factors other than 1 (like the examples above) are called primitive Pythagorean triples.

Real-World Applications

The Pythagorean theorem isn't just for geometry class! It has many practical uses:

  • Construction & Architecture: Ensuring walls meet at a perfect 90° angle (squaring corners). Calculating roof slopes, diagonal supports, or the length of staircases.
  • Navigation & Surveying: Calculating the shortest distance between two points (as the crow flies). Used in GPS systems and mapping.
  • Design: Calculating dimensions in graphic design, engineering drawings, or even video game level design.
  • Physics & Engineering: Calculating resultant vectors (e.g., forces acting at right angles), analyzing trajectories, or determining distances in spatial problems.
  • Everyday Life: Determining if a piece of furniture will fit through a doorway diagonally, calculating the length of a ladder needed to reach a certain height, or even in sports for field dimensions.

Common Mistakes & Study Tips

Common Mistakes:

  • Applying to Non-Right Triangles: Remember, the theorem ONLY works for triangles with a 90° angle.
  • Mixing Up Sides: Always identify the hypotenuse (c) correctly – it's the side opposite the right angle and the longest side. a and b are the legs.
  • Forgetting to Square Root: After finding c² (or a² or b²), you need to take the square root to find the actual length of the side c (or a or b).
  • Calculation Errors: Double-check your squaring and addition/subtraction.
  • Confusing Area with Side Length: a² is the area of a square with side a, not the length of side a itself.

Study Tips:

  • Draw Diagrams: Always sketch the triangle and label the sides you know and the side you need to find.
  • Identify the Hypotenuse First: This is the most critical step.
  • Write Down the Formula: Start each problem by writing a² + b² = c².
  • Rearrange Before Substituting (Optional but helpful): If finding a leg, rearrange the formula first (e.g., a² = c² - b²) before plugging in numbers.
  • Practice with Pythagorean Triples: Recognizing common triples can save time.
  • Use the Calculator Here: Check your manual calculations with the interactive calculator on this page.
  • Test Yourself: Use the quiz section regularly to reinforce your understanding.
  • Understand the Converse: Know how to use the theorem to check if a triangle is right-angled.
